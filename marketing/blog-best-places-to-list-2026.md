# Best Places to List Your Indie SaaS in 2026

*So you've built something. Now what? Here's an honest breakdown of where indie tools actually get discovered in 2026 -- what works, what's changed, and where to spend your time.*

---

## The Landscape Has Shifted

Two years ago the playbook was simple: launch on Product Hunt, post on Hacker News, submit to a few directories, done. That doesn't work anymore. Product Hunt is dominated by VC-backed companies with marketing budgets. Hacker News is a lottery. And Google SEO takes months to kick in.

Meanwhile, AI agents are becoming a major discovery channel. When someone asks Claude or ChatGPT "what's a good open-source analytics tool?", the answer comes from Reddit threads, blog posts, GitHub READMEs, and tool directories -- not from Google ads. If your tool isn't mentioned in those places, it doesn't exist to AI.

Here's where you should actually be listing in 2026, ranked by real impact for indie makers.

## Tier 1: High Impact, Do These First

### 1. IndieStack
**What it is:** A curated directory specifically for indie and open-source developer tools. 350+ tools across 21 categories.
**Why it matters:** Unlike generic directories, IndieStack is built for indie tools -- so you're not competing with Salesforce and HubSpot for visibility. It has an MCP server, which means AI agents can query the directory in real time. When someone's AI assistant searches for "best indie authentication tool", IndieStack's database is one of the places it looks. Makers can claim listings, track analytics, and sell directly through the marketplace.
**Cost:** Free listing. Optional 29 GBP Boost for featured placement.
**Link:** [indiestack.fly.dev](https://indiestack.fly.dev)

### 2. Product Hunt
**What it is:** The OG launch platform. Still has massive reach.
**Why it's complicated:** It works, but the game has changed. You need to coordinate upvotes, have a strong maker network, and ideally launch on a weekday when competition is lower. The "top 5 of the day" badge still carries weight, but getting there as a solo founder is harder than ever.
**Best for:** Big launches with a coordinated push. Not great for quiet, steady discovery.
**Cost:** Free.
**Link:** [producthunt.com](https://www.producthunt.com)

### 3. Hacker News (Show HN)
**What it is:** The developer community. A front-page post can send thousands of qualified visitors in hours.
**Why it's a gamble:** You have zero control over whether your post gets traction. The community is allergic to marketing. But when it works, it really works -- and HN threads are heavily indexed by AI models.
**Best for:** Open-source tools, developer-focused products, technical audiences.
**Cost:** Free.
**Link:** [news.ycombinator.com](https://news.ycombinator.com)

## Tier 2: Strong Directories

### 4. AlternativeTo
**What it is:** "Alternatives to X" -- users search for a tool and see indie alternatives.
**Why it works:** Incredibly high intent traffic. Someone searching "Notion alternatives" is actively looking to switch. AlternativeTo pages rank well on Google AND get cited by AI agents.
**Cost:** Free.

### 5. SaaSHub
**What it is:** SaaS comparison and discovery platform.
**Why it works:** Good SEO, comparison features, and the "trending" section can drive sustained traffic.
**Cost:** Free listing, paid promotion available.

### 6. BetaList
**What it is:** Directory for early-stage startups and beta products.
**Why it works:** Good for pre-launch and early traction. The audience is early adopters who are willing to try new things.
**Cost:** Free (slow queue) or paid for faster listing.

### 7. GitHub (if open-source)
**What it is:** Not a directory, but your README is one of the most important "listings" you have.
**Why it works:** GitHub READMEs are heavily indexed by AI models. A good README with clear positioning shows up in agent recommendations. Stars are social proof. Issues and discussions create content that AI agents reference.
**Cost:** Free.

## Tier 3: Worth Submitting To

### 8. Indie Hackers
**What it is:** Community for indie makers. Product directory plus forums.
**Why it works:** Engaged audience of exactly the right people. The community aspect means your product gets discussed, not just listed.
**Cost:** Free.

### 9. SourceForge
**What it is:** One of the oldest software directories. Still has incredible domain authority.
**Why it works:** SEO. SourceForge pages rank for everything. A listing here helps your tool show up in search results and AI training data.
**Cost:** Free.

### 10. StackShare
**What it is:** "What tech stack does X company use?" -- tools listed alongside real company stacks.
**Why it works:** High credibility signal when companies list your tool as part of their stack.
**Cost:** Free.

### 11. G2 / Capterra
**What it is:** Enterprise software review platforms.
**Why it works:** Reviews carry serious weight for B2B. But the audience skews enterprise, so this is less relevant for pure indie tools.
**Cost:** Free listing, paid for promotion.

## Tier 4: The Long Tail

These won't individually drive much traffic, but collectively they build the web of mentions that AI agents learn from:

- **Awesome Lists on GitHub** -- find the relevant awesome-* repo for your category and submit a PR
- **dev.to / Hashnode** -- write about building your tool, cross-post everywhere
- **Reddit** -- r/SideProject, r/indiehackers, r/selfhosted, r/nocode. Don't spam. Be helpful, mention your tool when relevant
- **F6S** -- startup directory, good for early stage
- **Crunchbase** -- adds legitimacy, AI agents reference it
- **MicroConf Connect** -- if you're a bootstrapper, this community is gold

## The AI Discovery Strategy

Here's the thing most people miss: traditional SEO is becoming table stakes. The new game is **AI discovery** -- making sure that when someone asks an AI agent for a tool recommendation, yours comes up.

AI agents learn from:
1. **Reddit threads** where your tool is mentioned naturally
2. **GitHub READMEs** and issue discussions
3. **Blog posts** that compare tools in your category
4. **Tool directories** that agents can query (like IndieStack's MCP server)
5. **Hacker News** discussions

The tools that get listed in these places NOW are the ones that AI agents will recommend for years. It's compound interest -- every mention makes the next recommendation more likely.

## The Realistic Playbook

If you're a solo founder with limited time, here's the priority order:

1. **List on IndieStack** -- takes 2 minutes, gets you into AI agent discovery immediately
2. **Polish your GitHub README** -- this is your most important asset for AI discovery
3. **Submit to AlternativeTo and SaaSHub** -- high intent traffic, minimal effort
4. **Post a Show HN** -- lottery ticket but high upside
5. **Be active on Reddit** -- answer questions in your niche, mention your tool when relevant
6. **Submit to 5-10 directories from the long tail** -- 30 minutes of work, permanent backlinks
7. **Write one comparison blog post** -- "X vs Y vs Z" for your category

Skip the things that feel like marketing and focus on the things that feel like being helpful. That's what works in 2026.
