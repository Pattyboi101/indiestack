# I Listed My Tool on 10 Directories -- Here's What Actually Drove Traffic

*Not all directories are equal. After listing an indie SaaS tool on 10 different platforms, here's the honest breakdown of what sent real users and what was a waste of time.*

---

## The Experiment

When you launch an indie tool, the standard advice is "submit to every directory you can find." So that's what we did. We took a real indie developer tool and listed it on 10 platforms over 2 weeks, then tracked the results for 30 days.

We tracked: unique visitors, signups, time on site, and whether the traffic was actually relevant (developers, not bots or random clicks).

Here's what happened.

---

## The Results (Ranked by Actual Impact)

### 1. Reddit (organic mentions) -- 40% of total traffic
**Not technically a directory, but the biggest driver by far.**

We didn't spam links. We answered questions in relevant subreddits (r/selfhosted, r/nocode, r/SideProject) and mentioned the tool when it was genuinely relevant. Three comments got upvoted and together drove more traffic than every directory combined.

**Key insight:** One authentic Reddit comment in a popular thread outperforms most directory listings. The traffic is high quality too -- these are developers actively discussing the problem your tool solves.

**Effort:** Medium. You need to actually be helpful, not just drop links.

### 2. IndieStack -- 22% of total traffic
**The surprise performer.**

[IndieStack](https://indiestack.fly.dev) is a curated directory specifically for indie developer tools. The traffic was lower volume than Reddit but incredibly high quality -- almost all visitors were developers actively browsing for tools in our category.

What made it different from other directories:
- Category pages rank well and people actually browse them
- The comparison feature means people see your tool alongside alternatives
- The MCP server means AI agents recommend tools from the directory
- We claimed the listing and added screenshots which boosted click-through

**Effort:** Low. 5 minutes to claim, 10 minutes to add screenshots and description.

### 3. AlternativeTo -- 15% of total traffic
**High intent, steady traffic.**

People on AlternativeTo are searching for "alternatives to X" -- they've already decided to switch from something. If your tool is an alternative to a popular product, this is gold.

The traffic trickled in slowly but consistently. Still getting visits 30 days later.

**Effort:** Low. Submit and wait.

### 4. Hacker News (Show HN) -- 12% of total traffic
**Massive spike, then nothing.**

The Show HN post got moderate traction (45 upvotes). Traffic spiked for 24 hours then dropped to near zero. But those visitors were highly engaged -- best time-on-site of any source.

**Key insight:** HN is a lottery. The post could have gotten 0 upvotes or 500. You can't control it. But the thread lives forever and AI agents reference HN discussions heavily.

**Effort:** Low to submit, but you need to monitor and respond to comments.

### 5. Product Hunt -- 6% of total traffic
**Disappointing for the effort involved.**

We launched on a Tuesday, coordinated upvotes, had a nice product page. Ended up #8 for the day. The traffic was decent on launch day but dropped off immediately and the visitors were mostly other makers, not potential users.

**Key insight:** Product Hunt is great for awareness and social proof (you can say "featured on Product Hunt") but the actual user acquisition is minimal unless you hit top 3.

**Effort:** High. Coordinating a PH launch takes days of preparation.

### 6. SaaSHub -- 3% of total traffic
**Slow but steady.**

Similar to AlternativeTo but lower volume. The comparison features are nice and the pages rank well on Google.

**Effort:** Low.

### 7-10. BetaList, SourceForge, G2, Capterra -- 2% combined
**Negligible direct traffic, but...**

These barely sent any visitors directly. However, they serve two purposes:
1. **Backlinks** -- these are high domain authority sites. Each listing improves your SEO.
2. **AI training data** -- every mention of your tool on a reputable site increases the chance AI agents recommend it.

Don't expect traffic from these. Think of them as long-term investments in discoverability.

**Effort:** Low to medium. G2 and Capterra require more setup.

---

## What We Learned

### The 80/20 of directory listings:
- **Reddit + IndieStack + AlternativeTo** drove 77% of all traffic
- **Everything else combined** drove 23%
- **Time spent on PH launch** was disproportionate to results

### Quality vs quantity:
- IndieStack and AlternativeTo sent the most qualified traffic (developers actively looking for tools)
- Product Hunt and HN sent more tire-kickers
- Reddit traffic quality depended entirely on which thread it came from

### The AI factor:
We noticed something unexpected. About a month after listing on IndieStack and having a few Reddit threads mention the tool, AI agents started recommending it. When asked "what's a good [category] tool?", Claude and ChatGPT began including it in their suggestions.

This is the compounding effect: directory listings + Reddit mentions + GitHub presence = AI agents learn about your tool = more recommendations = more traffic. IndieStack's MCP server accelerates this because agents can query it in real time rather than relying on training data.

### What we'd do differently:
1. **Start with IndieStack and AlternativeTo** -- low effort, high quality traffic
2. **Invest in Reddit presence** -- be genuinely helpful in relevant communities
3. **Skip the PH launch coordination** -- just submit and don't stress about ranking
4. **Submit to the long-tail directories** for backlinks, don't check traffic from them
5. **Focus on GitHub README** -- the indirect traffic from AI recommendations is massive

## The Realistic Timeline

- **Day 1:** List on [IndieStack](https://indiestack.fly.dev) (5 mins), AlternativeTo (10 mins), SaaSHub (10 mins)
- **Day 2:** Submit to BetaList, SourceForge, StackShare (30 mins total)
- **Week 1:** Start being active in relevant Reddit communities (ongoing)
- **Week 2:** Submit a Show HN when you have something interesting to show
- **Week 3:** If you have bandwidth, do a Product Hunt launch
- **Ongoing:** Answer questions, mention your tool when relevant, let the compound effect work

Total time investment: about 4 hours upfront, then 20-30 minutes per day on community engagement.

The directories do the passive work. You do the active work on Reddit and GitHub. Together, they build the presence that AI agents learn from.
